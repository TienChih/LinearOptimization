    Section 6.1 Min-Max Games

    Exploration 6.1.1.

    Suppose we have two players, then “Even” player and the “Odd” player. Each player picks an integer from 1-3.
    • If the sum is even:
      • If the chosen numbers are distinct, then the Odd player pays the Even player the difference between the numbers.
      • If the chosen numbers are the same, then the Odd player pays the Even player the sum of the numbers.
    • If the sum is odd: the Even player pays the Odd player $3.

    (a)

    Record the net winnings to the Even player in the following table:
    Odd
    \(1\) \(2\) \(3\)
    \(1\) \(?\) \(?\) \(?\)
    Even \(2\) \(?\) \(?\) \(?\)
    \(3\) \(?\) \(?\) \(?\)

    (b)

    Examinine this table, and comparing the rows, is there any advantage to the Even player in picking the first row over the third row?

    (c)

    Comparing the columns, is there any advantage to the Odd player in picking the third column over the first column?

    (d)

    If the Even player always picks a 2, what is the optimal strategy for the Odd player? Similarly if the Odd player always picks a 1, what is the optimal strategy for the even player?

    Do either player gain any advantage by picking a single choice and sticking to it?

    (f)

    (h)

    Definition 6.1.1.

    In a two-player zero-sum game, where the row player has \(n\) choices and the column player has \(m\) choices, the payoff matrix is a matrix which records in each row and column the net payoff to the row player (this choice is purely by convention, but we will stick to it.)
    If a row \(i\) has entries that are strictly greater than or equal to the entries of another row \(j\text{,}\) then we say that row \(i\) dominates row \(j\text{.}\) We then may delete row \(j\) since there is now reason the row player would choose \(j\text{.}\) Similarly, if column \(i\) is less than or equal to column \(j\text{,}\) column \(i\) dominates column \(j\) and we may delete column \(j\text{.}\)

    Activity 6.1.2.

    Consider the payoff matrix for a game between Rowan and Colleen.
    Colleen
    \(a_{11}\) \(a_{12}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{1m}\)
    Rowan \(a_{21}\) \(a_{22}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{2m}\)
    \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\)
    \(a_{n1}\) \(a_{n2}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{nm}\)

    (a)

    Suppose that Rowan pursues a mixed strategy a probability distribution of their choices \(P\) where
    \begin{equation*} P^\top = \begin{bmatrix} p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_n\end{bmatrix}, p_i\geq 0, \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i=1. \end{equation*}
    If Colleen chooses column \(j\) as her strategy, what is \(E_j(P)\) the expected value of Rowan’s earnings?
    1. \(E_j(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}\text{.}\)
    2. \(E_j(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\text{.}\)
    3. \(E_j(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}p_i\text{.}\)
    4. \(E_j(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}\text{.}\)
    5. \(E_j(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\text{.}\)
    6. \(E_j(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}p_i\text{.}\)

    (b)

    If Colleen somehow figures out Rowan’s strategy \(P\text{,}\) which column \(j\) should she choose?

    (c)

    Rowan is aware that Colleen is a sharp player can likely figure out \(P\text{.}\) How then should he choose his strategy? \(P\text{?}\)
    1. Maximize \(u=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq i\leq n}E_j(P)\text{.}\)
    2. Maximize \(u=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq i\leq n}E_j(P)\text{.}\)
    3. Minimize \(u=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq i\leq n}E_j(P)\text{.}\)
    4. Minimize \(u=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq i\leq n}E_j(P)\text{.}\)

    (d)

    At the exact same time, Colleen is pursuing her own mixed strategy \(Q\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} Q = \begin{bmatrix} q_1 \amp q_2 \amp \cdots \amp q_m \end{bmatrix}, q_j\geq 0, \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j=1. \end{equation*}
    If Rowan chooses row \(i\) as his strategy, what is \(F_i(Q)\) the expected value of Colleen’s losses?
    1. \(v=F_i(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}\text{.}\)
    2. \(v=F_i(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j\text{.}\)
    3. \(v=F_i(P) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}q_j\text{.}\)
    4. \(v=F_i(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}\text{.}\)
    5. \(v=F_i(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j\text{.}\)
    6. \(v=F_i(P) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}q_j\text{.}\)

    (e)

    If Roawn somehow figures out Colleen’s strategy \(Q\text{,}\) which row \(i\) should he choose?

    (f)

    Colleen is also aware that Rowan is a sharp player can likely figure out \(Q\text{.}\) How then should she choose her strategy? \(Q\text{?}\)
    1. Maximize \(\displaystyle \max_{1\leq j\leq m}F_i(Q)\text{.}\)
    2. Maximize \(\displaystyle \min_{1\leq j\leq m}F_i(Q)\text{.}\)
    3. Minimize \(\displaystyle \max_{1\leq j\leq m}F_i(Q)\text{.}\)
    4. Minimize \(\displaystyle \min_{1\leq j\leq m}F_i(Q)\text{.}\)

    (g)

    Consider the primal-dual program encoded by the tableau:
    \(\bar{v}\) \(q_1\) \(q_1\) \(\cdots\) \(q_m\) \(-1\)
    \(\bar{u}\) \(0\) \(-1\) \(-1\) \(\cdots\) \(-1\) \(-1\) \(=-0\)
    \(p_1\) \(-1\) \(a_{11}\) \(a_{12}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{1m}\) \(0\) \(=-t_1\)
    \(p_2\) \(-1\) \(a_{21}\) \(a_{22}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{2m}\) \(0\) \(=-t_2\)
    \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
    \(p_n\) \(-1\) \(a_{n1}\) \(a_{n2}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{nm}\) \(0\) \(=-t_n\)
    \(-1\) \(-1\) \(0\) \(0\) \(\cdots\) \(0\) \(0\) \(=f\)
    \(=0\) \(=s_1\) \(=s_2\) \(\cdots\) \(=s_n\) \(=g\)
    Write out both the primal and dual problems encoded by this tableau. How do they relate to the Rowan and Colleen’s strategies?

    (h)

    Show that the \(p_i, q_j\) satisfy the conditions for \(P, Q,\) to be mixed strategies.

    (i)

    In solving this system, some rows or columns may be deleted. Why is this okay?

    Definition 6.1.2.

    Suppose that the reduced payoff matrix had an entry \(a_{ij}\) that is the largest value in it’s column and the smallest value in it’s row.
    \(\bar{v}\) \(q_k\) \(\cdots\) \(q_j\) \(-1\)
    \(\bar{u}\) \(0\) \(-1\) \(\cdots\) \(-1\) \(-1\) \(=-0\)
    \(p_\ell\) \(-1\) \(a_{\ell k}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{\ell j}\) \(0\) \(=-t_\ell\)
    \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
    \(p_i\) \(-1\) \(a_{ik}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{ij}\) \(0\) \(=-t_i\)
    \(-1\) \(-1\) \(0\) \(\cdots\) \(0\) \(0\) \(=f\)
    \(=0\) \(=s_k\) \(\cdots\) \(=s_j\) \(=g\)
    Such an entry \(a_{ij}\) is called a saddle point.
