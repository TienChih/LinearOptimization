Activity 5.1.3.

Consider the payoff matrix for a game between Rowan and Colleen.
Colleen
\(a_{11}\) \(a_{12}\) \(\cdots\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{1m}\)
\(a_{21}\) \(a_{22}\) \(\cdots\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{2m}\)
Rowan \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\)
\(a_{n1}\) \(a_{n2}\) \(\cdots\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{nm}\)

(a)

Suppose that Rowan pursues a mixed strategy a probability distribution of their choices \(\p\) where
\begin{equation*} \p^\top = \begin{bmatrix} p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_n\end{bmatrix}, p_i\geq 0, \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i=1. \end{equation*}
If Colleen chooses column \(j\) as her strategy, what is \(E_j(\p)\) the expected value of Rowan’s earnings?
  1. \(E_j(\p) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}\text{.}\)
  2. \(E_j(\p) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\text{.}\)
  3. \(E_j(\p) = \displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}p_i\text{.}\)
  4. \(E_j(\p) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}\text{.}\)
  5. \(E_j(\p) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\text{.}\)
  6. \(E_j(\p) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ij}p_i\text{.}\)

(b)

If Colleen somehow figures out Rowan’s strategy \(\p\text{,}\) which column \(j\) should she choose?

(c)

Rowan is aware that Colleen is a sharp player can likely figure out \(\p\text{.}\) How then should he choose his strategy? \(\p\text{?}\)
  1. Maximize \(u=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq j\leq m}E_j(\p)\text{.}\)
  2. Maximize \(u=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq j\leq m}E_j(\p)\text{.}\)
  3. Minimize \(u=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq j\leq m}E_j(\p)\text{.}\)
  4. Minimize \(u=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq j\leq m}E_j(\p)\text{.}\)

(d)

At the exact same time, Colleen is pursuing her own mixed strategy \(\q\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \q = \begin{bmatrix} q_1 \amp q_2 \amp \cdots \amp q_m \end{bmatrix}, q_j\geq 0, \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j=1. \end{equation*}
If Rowan chooses row \(i\) as his strategy, what is \(F_i(\q)\) the expected value of Colleen’s losses?
  1. \(F_i(\q) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}\text{.}\)
  2. \(F_i(\q) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j\text{.}\)
  3. \(F_i(\q) = \displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}q_j\text{.}\)
  4. \(F_i(\q) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}\text{.}\)
  5. \(F_i(\q) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m q_j\text{.}\)
  6. \(F_i(\q) = \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^m a_{ij}q_j\text{.}\)

(e)

If Rowan somehow figures out Colleen’s strategy \(\q\text{,}\) which row \(i\) should he choose?

(f)

Colleen is also aware that Rowan is a sharp player can likely figure out \(\q\text{.}\) How then should she choose her strategy? \(\q\text{?}\)
  1. Maximize \(v=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq i\leq n}F_i(\q)\text{.}\)
  2. Maximize \(v=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq i\leq n}F_i(\q)\text{.}\)
  3. Minimize \(v=\displaystyle \max_{1\leq i\leq n}F_i(\q)\text{.}\)
  4. Minimize \(v=\displaystyle \min_{1\leq i\leq n}F_i(\q)\text{.}\)

(g)

Consider the primal-dual problems encoded by the tableau:

\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\ddots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
\(p_n\) \(-1\) \(a_{n1}\) \(a_{n2}\) \(\cdots\) \(a_{nm}\) \(0\) \(=-t_n\)
\(-1\) \(-1\) \(0\) \(0\) \(\cdots\) \(0\) \(0\) \(=f\)
\(=0\) \(=s_1\) \(=s_2\) \(\cdots\) \(=s_n\) \(=g\)
Write out both the primal and dual problems encoded by this tableau. (Including all equalities, inequalities, and the objective functions)
